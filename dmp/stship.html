NERC-funded PhD studentship at UCL
Evolution in tropical butterflies 
Enthusiastic, talented and adventurous students with strong interests in evolution, genetics, or ecology are invited to apply for this studentship on evolution in tropical butterflies.  The work will involve a mixture of fieldwork in South and Central American rainforests, molecular genetics, and data analysis.  I am seeking an independent-minded candidate who will fit in with our research group and collaborators in Panama, the Natural History Museum, and elsewhere. The precise project depends on the particular student's interests and strengths, but will be among the following:
 
1) Molecular phylogeny of Oleria.  Many taxa have been described in this genus of small, mimetic, often transparent butterflies (Ithomiinae, see background) of the rainforest understory. However, very little is known about the biology or systematics of Oleria; it remains unclear exactly how many species exist (probably around 40).  Diversity in the genus peaks in the Andes and nearby Amazonian lowlands of Ecuador and Peru.  There is also a high degree of local differentiation into racial or semi-species forms, making Oleria an excellent model system for studying speciation. The project would involve fieldwork and and analysis of DNA sequences to establish relationships between the taxa.  The research complements that of Dr. Keith Willmott, who will be working on species delimitation and morphological systematics of Oleria, funded by a Leverhulme grant.  Dr. Willmott will work in Ecuador and at the London Natural History Museum for 3 years from 2001. We propose to use comparative methods to test Bates' (1862) theory that shifts in mimicry can result in speciation. If colour patterns used in mimicry also act as mating cues (see below), mimetic butterflies may provide an example where ecological adaptations promote speciation.

2) Mimicry and speciation in Heliconius.  Our group is investigating a pair of Panamanian sister species, Heliconius cydno and H. melpomene.  The two species differ in their colour pattern due to Müllerian mimicry, and we have shown that colour is also important in mate recognition (Jiggins et al., Nature, in press).

 Mating behaviour between the species in sympatry is more choosy than between allopatric populations, suggesting that speciation  has occurred by "reinforcement", but we need more experimental work from various allopatric and sympatric populations to test whether this effect is general.  The species occasionally hybridize in nature, and we will carry out field experiments to test whether the hybrid colour patterns are selected against because of a lack of mimciry. Finally, laboratory hybrids between the species produce fertile males and sterile females; to our surprise, we also find unisexual sterility and non-random mating between Guiana and Panama races of one of the species, H. melpomene.  Further work is required to find the contact zone, probably in Venezuela between the two semisterile groups of H. melpomene. The student would join an interdisciplinary team, including Dr. Chris Jiggins, Dr. Biff Bermingham at STRI, Panama, and another UCL student, Vanessa Bull, currently based at STRI.

3) Biogeography of the foothills of the Andes.  The worlds' greatest diversity in plants, insects, and birds is found in the rainforests of the Eastern foothills of the Andes. The steep ecological gradients with altitude, temperature and humidity in this region mean that many closely related taxa meet, overlap, and sometimes hybridize. To what extent can the history of the biota be deduced from today's complex patterns of distribution?  The student will study the distribution and genetics of Heliconius and/or Ithomiinae to examine these questions in NE Peru and Ecuador.
